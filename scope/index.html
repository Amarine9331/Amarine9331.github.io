// scope

A deep-dive into the research questions driving my work, the biological scales I navigate, and the computational methodologies I build to make sense of complex ecosystems.

// systems across scales

The biology I care about is fundamentally multi-scale. The same perturbation that flips a transcript on or off inside a single cell propagates outward into cellular behavior and, eventually, into the composition and output of an entire community. Understanding any one scale in isolation is incomplete; the interesting questions live in the seams.

Molecular layer

The genome, transcriptome, proteome, and metabolome — the molecular state executing cellular function. Using high-resolution profiling (long-read sequencing, mass spectrometry, and single-cell multi-omics), I integrate and model high-dimensional molecular readouts to trace functional states.

Cellular layer

The integrated phenotype of an individual cell or organism — how molecular cascades translate to growth, stress tolerance, metabolic yield, and lineage-specific traits. This is where genotype meets environment, and where our molecular models face physical validation.

Community layer

Interacting populations in ecological or host-associated niches. Thousands of distinct lineages co-occur, exchange metabolites, and respond collectively to perturbations. Emergent dynamics dominate this scale, making prediction both challenging and highly rewarding.

The questions I find most compelling cross these layers — predicting community-level behavior from molecular state, or inferring molecular determinants from community-level observations. The same data, read at a different scale, often tells a different story.

// research thrusts

Microbial stress response

Environmental stressors induce rapid transcriptional, metabolic, and compositional shifts that leave ecological signatures in microbial systems. I study which functional products emerge under stress, how long they persist, and the precise timescales of disturbance and recovery. My goal is to discover whether these responses are generalizable — whether a "stress signature" learned in one system predicts dynamics in another. This work integrates time-resolved multi-omics with mechanistic and statistical models of community behavior.

Linking function to (meta)phenome

Stress events reshape the genomic context of microbial populations, selecting for specific mobile elements, accessory genes, and horizontally transferred loci. I map these genomic features to observable community phenotypes to identify which lineages, gene clusters, and pathways drive shifts in metabolic output and growth. By anchoring abstract gene annotations to concrete, measurable phenome traits, I seek to predict exactly how a community behaves in a given environment based on its genetic repertoire.

Bioprospecting following stress response

I discover and characterize novel genes, enzymes, and small molecules that confer stress tolerance or accelerate ecosystem recovery. Using comparative multi-omics, I identify candidate loci enriched during environmental pulses and validate them experimentally via heterologous expression and quantitative assays. This integrative pipeline bridges ecological resilience and synthetic biology, accelerating natural product discovery, bioremediation, and biotechnological innovation.

Computational and ML methodology for biological inference

Cross-cutting these domains, I build computational frameworks — integrating statistical models, phylogenetic structures, foundation models, and machine learning — to unify multi-omic and phenotypic datasets. I focus on developing methods that scale across systems, remain honest about biological uncertainty, and respect data-specific structures (compositional, sparse, or hierarchical). My priority is creating interpretable models that yield biological insights rather than just maximizing performance benchmarks.
