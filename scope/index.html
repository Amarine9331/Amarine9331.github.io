// scope

A longer-form description of the research questions I'm working on, the biological scales I move between, and the methodology I rely on.

// systems across scales

The biology I care about is fundamentally multi-scale. The same perturbation that flips a transcript on or off inside a single cell propagates outward into cellular behavior and, eventually, into the composition and output of an entire community. Understanding any one scale in isolation is incomplete; the interesting questions live in the seams.

Molecular layer

The genome, transcriptome, proteome, and metabolome — the molecular state that encodes and executes cellular function. Modern profiling (long- and short-read sequencing, mass spectrometry, single-cell methods) lets us read out this layer with increasing depth and resolution. Most of my computational work starts here: cleaning, integrating, and modeling molecular readouts.

Cellular layer

The integrated phenotype of an individual cell or organism — how molecular state propagates into observable behavior, growth, stress tolerance, metabolic output, and lineage-specific traits. This is where genotype starts to make contact with environment, and where the assumptions baked into molecular models get tested.

Community layer

Interacting populations in ecological or host-associated contexts. Microbial communities are the canonical example: thousands of distinct lineages co-occurring, exchanging metabolites, and responding collectively to disturbance. Behavior at this scale is rarely the sum of its parts — emergent dynamics dominate, and that’s what makes prediction hard and interesting.

The questions I find most compelling cross these layers — predicting community-level behavior from molecular state, or inferring molecular determinants from community-level observations. The same data, read at a different scale, often tells a different story.

// research thrusts

Microbial stress response

Environmental stressors induce rapid transcriptional, metabolic, and compositional shifts that can leave short-term ecological memory within microbial communities. I'm interested in quantifying which functional products appear under stress, how long they persist, and what timescales communities follow as they transition through disturbance and recovery. A central question is whether these responses are predictable and generalizable across systems and perturbations — whether a "stress signature" learned on one community transfers to another. This work integrates time-resolved multi-omic profiling with mechanistic and statistical models of community dynamics.

Linking function to (meta)phenome

Stress events reshape the genomic context of microbial populations — composition, mobile elements, accessory genes, and horizontally transferred loci that mediate adaptation. I'm interested in how these features map onto observable community phenotypes: which lineages, gene clusters, and pathways drive measurable shifts in metabolite output, growth, and ecological role during environmental pulses. The goal is to anchor abstract functional annotations to concrete, measurable phenome-level traits — so that "this community has gene X" becomes "this community is more likely to do Y under Z."

Bioprospecting following stress response

A tertiary aim is to discover and characterize novel genes, enzymes, and small molecules that confer stress tolerance or facilitate rapid recovery in their host or ecosystem. Through comparative multi-omics, I identify candidate loci and products enriched during stress pulses and validate them experimentally via heterologous expression and quantitative assays. This integrative pipeline bridges discovery and application — linking ecological resilience to synthetic biology, natural product discovery, bioremediation, and biotechnological innovation.

Computational and ML methodology for biological inference

Cross-cutting the thrusts above: I build and apply computational methods — statistical models, phylogenetic frameworks, foundation models, and modern ML — to integrate multi-omic, phylogenetic, and phenotypic data. The goal is methodology that scales across biological systems and lets predictions from one community or perturbation generalize to the next. I care about methods that are honest about uncertainty, that respect the structure of the data they're trained on (compositional, phylogenetic, sparse), and that produce interpretable handles for biologists, not just leaderboard numbers.

// collaboration

I’m always interested in collaborations that span scales or that pair computational methodology with experimental validation.
